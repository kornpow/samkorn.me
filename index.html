Photon Home

These are the design revisions of the PhotonHome, a Particle Photon powered outlet controller. Why invest in all-new smart home electronics, when you can just make all of your existing things work smarter, for you! Here is the Hack-A-Day Link. You'd think I could figure out HTML, but I just can't for the life of me make this link clickable.

https://hackaday.io/project/6229-photonhome-ac-power-iot-homebase

This first image is not even the PhotonHome device. This is my most recent PCB as of January 25th, and it is coming soon from OSHPark. It is basically the final demo sensor for this product. It has inputs for Thermocouple, DHT22 Temperature and Humidity sensor, 2 RS2412B "Neopixel" outputs, and will take in power using a 3A@5V AC/DC converter to power the LEDs and everything else.



This is the latest PhotonHome PCB. This board is when I transistioned from it being an ATmega328p board to a WiFi controllable Photon board. I have really come to learn to appreciate the dev-board. I really sometimes get carried away in trying to choose components and dive right in, but a better solution is the faster solution, and the Photon had everything that I needed in one package. You should really just check out my HackADay! Link-please-work!



This board is the last of the DriverDuino series, DriverDuino V3 which is what the project was before I switched to the Photon. New in this board is PCB mounted relays, bluetooth, real-time clock. Not all of those things however did work, or would have worked... I took this project up for my Digital Design Lab class, when I needed a quick pivot, because my other project was way to big.



DriverDuino V2. A relic just for reference. Never actually sent off for manufacture, but designed to fixed the idiotic flaws of DriverDuino V1. Featured an ISP programming header (the last one lacked that), in addition to a FTDI serial port.



DriverDuino V1, the original. Designed with an instructable in mind for controlling an electrical outlet with relays. I forgot the programming header and only had the FTDI header, so could never get the Arduino bootloader on to actually program over serial. This was my first printed PCB for a personal project, and I have learned so much in the last year and a half.
